"I didn't know where to go," he burst out. "I came here earlier, but you weren't in."
"I dined late," I said.
I changed my mind: it was not liquor that had driven him to this obvious desperation. His face, 
usually so rosy, was now strangely mottled. His hands trembled.
"Has anything happened?" I asked.
"My wife has left me."
He could hardly get the words out. He gave a little gasp, and the tears began to trickle down his 
round cheeks. I did not know what to say. My first thought was that she had come to the end of her 
forbearance with his infatuation for Strickland, and, goaded by the latter's cynical behaviour, had 
insisted that he should be turned out. I knew her capable of temper, for all the calmness of her 
manner; and if Stroeve still refused, she might easily have flung out of the studio with vows never 
to return. But the little man was so distressed that I could not smile.
