of a mountain brook. Who can tell what anguished fancy made these fruits? They belonged to a 
Polynesian garden of the Hesperides. There was something strangely alive in them, as though they 
were created in a stage of the earth's dark history when things were not irrevocably fixed to their 
forms. They were extravagantly luxurious. They were heavy with tropical odours. They seemed to 
possess a sombre passion of their own. It was enchanted fruit, to taste which might open the 
gateway to God knows what secrets of the soul and to mysterious palaces of the imagination. They 
were sullen with unawaited dangers, and to eat them might turn a man to beast or god. All that was 
healthy and natural, all that clung to happy relationships and the simple joys of simple men, shrunk 
from them in dismay; and yet a fearful attraction was in them, and, like the fruit on the Tree of the
