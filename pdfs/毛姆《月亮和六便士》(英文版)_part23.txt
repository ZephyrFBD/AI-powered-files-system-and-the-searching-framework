myself with such aptness or with such fluency. In those days conversation was still cultivated as an 
art; a neat repartee was more highly valued than the crackling of thorns under a pot; and the 
epigram, not yet a mechanical appliance by which the dull may achieve a semblance of wit, gave 
sprightliness to the small talk of the urbane. It is sad that I can remember nothing of all this 
scintillation. But I think the conversation never settled down so comfortably as when it turned to 
the details of the trade which was the other side of the art we practised. When we had done 
discussing the merits of the latest book, it was natural to wonder how many copies had been sold, 
what advance the author had received, and how much he was likely to make out of it. Then we
