Mrs. Strickland's voice broke again.
"And what is to happen to them, poor darlings? How are we going to live?"
She struggled for self-control, and I saw her hands clench and unclench spasmodically. It was 
dreadfully painful.
"Of course I'll go over to Paris if you think I can do any good, but you must tell me exactly what 
you want me to do."
"I want him to come back."
"I understood from Colonel MacAndrew that you'd made up your mind to divorce him."
"I'll never divorce him," she answered with a sudden violence. "Tell him that from me. He'll never 
be able to marry that woman. I'm as obstinate as he is, and I'll never divorce him. I have to think of 
my children."
I think she added this to explain her attitude to me, but I thought it was due to a very natural 
jealousy rather than to maternal solicitude.
