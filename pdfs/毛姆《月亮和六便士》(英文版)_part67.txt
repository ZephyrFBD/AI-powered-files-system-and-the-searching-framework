She flushed darkly. I imagined that she saw her husband installed in a luxurious suite of rooms, 
dining at one smart restaurant after another, and she pictured his days spent at race-meetings and 
his evenings at the play.
"It can't go on at his age," she said. "After all, he's forty. I could understand it in a young man, but 
I think it's horrible in a man of his years, with children who are nearly grown up. His health will 
never stand it."
Anger struggled in her breast with misery.
"Tell him that our home cries out for him. Everything is just the same, and yet everything is 
different. I can't live without him. I'd sooner kill myself. Talk to him about the past, and all we've 
gone through together. What am I to say to the children when they ask for him? His room is 
exactly as it was when he left it. It's waiting for him. We're all waiting for him."
Now she told me exactly what I should say. She gave me elaborate answers to every possible 
observation of his.
