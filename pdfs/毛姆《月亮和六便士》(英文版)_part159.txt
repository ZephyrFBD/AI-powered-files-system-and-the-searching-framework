Stroeve had set up a 
Christmas-tree in his studio, and I suspected that we should both find absurd little presents hanging 
on its festive branches; but he was shy about seeing Strickland again; it was a little humiliating to 
forgive so easily insults so outrageous, and he wished me to be present at the reconciliation on 
which he was determined.
We walked together down the Avenue de Clichy, but Strickland was not in the cafe. It was too 
cold to sit outside, and we took our places on leather benches within. It was hot and stuffy, and the 
air was gray with smoke. Strickland did not come, but presently we saw the French painter who 
occasionally played chess with him. I had formed a casual acquaintance with him, and he sat down 
at our table. Stroeve asked him if he had seen Strickland.
"He's ill," he said. "Didn't you know?"
"Seriously?"
"Very, I understand."
Stroeve's face grew white.
