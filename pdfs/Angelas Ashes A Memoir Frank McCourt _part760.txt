 Going to work for Easons he is, working for that pack of freemasons 
 
 and Protestants above in Dublin.Too good for the post office but 
 
 ready and willing to deliver all kinds of filthy English magazines all over 
 
 Limerick.Every magazine he touches will be a mortal sin. But he’s leaving 
 
 now, so he is, and a sorry day it is for his poor mother that prayed 
 
 for a son with a pension to take care of her in her latter days. So here, 
 
 take your wages and go from the sight of us. 
 
 Miss Barry says, He’s a bad boy, isn’t he, boys? 
 
 He is, Miss Barry. 
 
 I don’t know what to say. I don’t know what I did wrong. Should I 
 
 say I’m sorry? Good-bye? 
 
 I lay my belt and pouch on Mrs. O’Connell’s desk. She glares at me, 
 
 Go on. Go to your job at Easons. Go from us. Next boy, come up for
