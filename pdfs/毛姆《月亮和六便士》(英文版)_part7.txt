lived obscurely. He made enemies rather than friends. It is not strange, then, that those who wrote 
of him should have eked out their scanty recollections with a lively fancy, and it is evident that 
there was enough in the little that was known of him to give opportunity to the romantic scribe; 
there was much in his life which was strange and terrible, in his character something outrageous, 
and in his fate not a little that was pathetic. In due course a legend arose of such circumstantiality 
that the wise historian would hesitate to attack it.
