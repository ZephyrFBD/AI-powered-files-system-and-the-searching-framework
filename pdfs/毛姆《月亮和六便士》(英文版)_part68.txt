I saw that she wished me to appeal to his sympathies by every means in my power. She was 
weeping freely. I was extraordinarily touched. I felt indignant at Strickland's cold cruelty, and I 
promised to do all I could to bring him back. I agreed to go over on the next day but one, and to 
stay in Paris till I had achieved something. Then, as it was growing late and we were both 
exhausted by so much emotion, I left her. 
Chapter XI
During the journey I thought over my errand with misgiving. Now that I was free from the 
spectacle of Mrs. Strickland's distress I could consider the matter more calmly. I was puzzled by 
the contradictions that I saw in her behaviour. She was very unhappy, but to excite my sympathy 
she was able to make a show of her unhappiness. It was evident that she had been prepared to
